Source: Kiirgusseadus (Radiation Act) RT I, 09.04.2019, 1;
        Draft nuclear power framework (seisuga 2024 — as of 2024, in development);
        IAEA Nuclear Security and Safety Review of Estonia (2022);
        Terviseamet (Health Board — radiation safety authority)
Authority: Terviseamet (Health Board), Majandus- ja Kommunikatsiooniministeerium, IAEA
Language: Estonian (summary in English for RAG indexing)

!!! REGULATORY WARNING — REGWARNING !!!
ESTONIA DOES NOT HAVE A DEDICATED NUCLEAR POWER PLANT LICENSING LAW AS OF 2024.
The Kiirgusseadus (Radiation Act) governs radiation sources and radioactive materials generally
but does NOT contain provisions for licensing a nuclear power reactor or SMR.
A dedicated nuclear energy law (Tuumaenergia seadus) is under development but was not yet
adopted as of the knowledge cutoff. Any SMR project in Estonia faces fundamental legal gaps.
Projects MUST NOT proceed past feasibility stage without confirming the legislative status.
All regulatory requirements below are indicative based on the draft framework and IAEA guidance.

=== PART 1: CURRENT LEGAL STATUS ===

Existing radiation safety legislation:
1. Kiirgusseadus (Radiation Act, RT I, 09.04.2019, 1): Governs use of ionizing radiation sources, radioactive materials, radiation protection, and nuclear material safeguards. Does NOT cover power reactor licensing.
2. Tuumamaterjalide ja radioaktiivsete jäätmete käitlemise seadus (Act on Handling Nuclear Materials and Radioactive Waste): Covers radioactive waste management and transport.
3. No Atomic Energy Act equivalent: Estonia has no analogue to Finland's Ydinenergia laki (990/1987), Sweden's Kärntekniklag (1984:3), or Germany's Atomgesetz.

Draft nuclear energy law (Tuumaenergia seadus):
- Under development by Majandus- ja Kommunikatsiooniministeerium (MKM)
- Draft concept paper circulated 2023; full draft law not yet published as of 2024
- Key elements expected in draft law:
  - Site siting license (asukoha luba)
  - Construction permit (ehitusluba — nuclear-specific provisions)
  - Operating license (tegevusluba)
  - Decommissioning permit
  - Radioactive waste fund obligation
  - Public interest determination (riigikogu otsus / parliamentary decision)
- Timeline: draft law expected for Riigikogu (Parliament) consideration 2025–2026
- Likelihood of adoption: uncertain; nuclear energy is politically sensitive in Estonia

Historical context:
- Soviet-era Latvia had a nuclear power plant (Ignalina NPP was in Lithuania, not Estonia)
- No nuclear power plants have ever operated in Estonia
- Estonia imports nuclear electricity from Finland and Sweden via Estlink interconnectors
- Public opinion on nuclear energy: mixed; Baltic security concerns add complexity
- Baltic states' energy security discussion (post-2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine) has revived nuclear interest

=== PART 2: CURRENT APPLICABLE FRAMEWORK (GAP ANALYSIS) ===

In the absence of a nuclear energy law, an SMR project would theoretically need to navigate:

A. Radiation Safety (Kiirgusseadus):
- Terviseamet (Health Board) issues radiation practice licenses
- Existing law covers: X-ray equipment, medical isotopes, industrial radiation sources, radioactive material transport
- NOT designed for: nuclear reactor fuel, reactor operation, nuclear safety systems
- Gap: No license category for nuclear power generation exists

B. Environmental Permitting (KMH-KSH seadus):
- Environmental Impact Assessment (KMH) would be mandatory for any nuclear facility
- KMH-KSH seadus §6: nuclear facilities are explicitly listed as requiring mandatory full EIA
- Competent authority: Keskkonnaamet; for nationally significant projects, Riigikogu vote may be required
- IAEA Environmental Impact Assessment guidance would apply

C. Building Permitting (Ehitusseadustik):
- Building permit (ehitusluba) required for construction of any building/structure
- No nuclear-specific provisions in Ehitusseadustik
- Gap: Nuclear safety-grade construction standards not referenced in Estonian building code

D. Energy Market (Elektrituruseadus):
- Electricity production license (ETS §14) required as for any generator
- No nuclear-specific provisions in ETS

E. Spatial Planning (Planeerimisseadus):
- Riigi eriplaneering (national spatial plan) would be required for nuclear facility
- Riigikogu (Parliament) vote on national significance classification likely needed
- Timeline for riigi eriplaneering: typically 5–8 years

F. International Obligations:
- IAEA membership: Estonia is a member of IAEA and party to NPT (Non-Proliferation Treaty)
- Joint Convention on Safety of Spent Fuel Management: Estonia is a signatory
- Convention on Nuclear Safety: Estonia is NOT currently a party (no nuclear power plants)
  — Accession would be required before nuclear power plant operation
- Nuclear and Radiation Safety Convention: being reviewed
- Euratom Treaty: Estonia is an EU member state and subject to Euratom Treaty
  (Chapter 4: supply of ores and nuclear materials; Chapter 7: safeguards)
- IAEA Safeguards Agreement: Estonia has Comprehensive Safeguards Agreement with IAEA (INFCIRC/695)
- Additional Protocol: Estonia has concluded IAEA Additional Protocol

=== PART 3: DRAFT REGULATORY PATHWAY (INDICATIVE) ===

Based on IAEA guidance (IAEA-TECDOC-1681: Licensing of advanced reactors; IAEA Safety Guide SSG-12)
and draft Estonian nuclear energy law concepts, the anticipated regulatory pathway would be:

Step 1 — National Policy Decision (Riigikogu):
- Riigikogu (Parliament) adopts a policy decision (otsus) authorizing nuclear power development
- This is the "principle decision" analogous to Finland's periaatepäätös
- Timeline: political process; potentially 2–4 years

Step 2 — Site Siting License (Asukoha luba):
- Granted by Terviseamet or new nuclear regulatory authority (yet to be established)
- Requirements: site safety analysis, geological survey, seismic hazard assessment, population density, coolant water availability
- EIA must be completed (Keskkonnaamet)
- Spatial plan adopted (riigi eriplaneering)
- Timeline: 3–5 years from decision to begin

Step 3 — Construction Permit (Ehitusluba / Tuumaluba):
- Issued by nuclear regulatory authority (Terviseamet or new body)
- Requirements: detailed design safety review, fuel supply contracts, waste management plan, financial security
- Compliance with IAEA safety standards (SSR-2/1 Rev.1 mandatory)
- Timeline: 2–4 years from siting license

Step 4 — Operating License (Tegevusluba):
- Issued after construction completion
- Pre-operational testing required (fuel load, low-power physics tests, power ascension tests)
- Timeline: 1–2 years

Step 5 — Decommissioning Plan:
- Required at time of operating license application
- Financial security for decommissioning must be demonstrated
- Radioactive waste management plan approved

=== PART 4: IAEA SAFETY STANDARDS APPLICABLE ===

Even without Estonian nuclear law, IAEA safety standards provide the international benchmark:

SSR-2/1 Rev.1 — Safety of Nuclear Power Plants: Design
- Fundamental safety design requirements for all nuclear power plants including SMRs
- Defence-in-depth principle, safety systems, redundancy and diversity requirements
- Chapter 6: Multiple barrier system (fuel matrix, fuel cladding, reactor coolant boundary, containment)

SSG-52 — Design of Reactor Coolant Systems and Associated Systems
- Coolant system design for light water reactors (most SMR designs)
- Passive safety systems (natural circulation, passive core cooling)

NS-R-5 Rev.1 — Safety of Research Reactors
- Applicable to smaller SMR designs similar in scale to research reactors

IAEA Safety Guide SSG-12:
- Site survey and evaluation for nuclear installations
- Geological, seismic, meteorological, and hydrological siting criteria

IAEA Nuclear Security Series No. 13:
- Nuclear security of nuclear facilities
- Physical protection requirements (Canberra Document, INFCIRC/225/Rev.6)

=== PART 5: TERVISEAMET (HEALTH BOARD) ===

Current nuclear-related functions of Terviseamet:
- Issues radiation practice licenses (kiirgustegevusluba) for existing radiation uses
- Supervises radiation safety in medical, industrial, and research applications
- Maintains national registry of radiation sources
- Coordinates with IAEA on safeguards reporting
- Does NOT currently have capacity to regulate nuclear power plant construction or operation
- Building capacity for nuclear oversight is being discussed in government but not yet funded

=== PART 6: SUMMARY OF REGULATORY GAPS (REGWARNING) ===

The following gaps must be addressed before any SMR project can proceed in Estonia:

Gap 1: No nuclear power law — CRITICAL
Status: Draft under development; adoption timeline uncertain (est. 2025–2026 earliest)
Impact: No legal basis for any nuclear construction or operation license

Gap 2: No nuclear regulatory authority — CRITICAL
Status: Terviseamet has limited nuclear capacity; no dedicated nuclear safety authority
Impact: No authority exists to review nuclear safety analyses or issue construction permit

Gap 3: No convention membership for nuclear power — HIGH
Status: Estonia not party to Convention on Nuclear Safety; accession needed
Impact: International obligations not met; EU peer review not available

Gap 4: No nuclear waste facility — HIGH
Status: No disposal facility for high-level radioactive waste in Estonia
Impact: Waste management strategy must be developed (potentially Baltic joint facility)

Gap 5: Public acceptance — MEDIUM
Status: Mixed public opinion; no national debate yet
Impact: Social license to operate uncertain

Gap 6: Grid size — MEDIUM
Status: Estonia's total installed electricity capacity ~1,700 MW; single SMR unit (300 MW) would be ~18% of total capacity
Impact: Grid stability analysis required; significant grid investment needed

REGWARNING CONCLUSION: Estonia is at the very early stage of nuclear energy consideration.
Any SMR project analysis must clearly note that the regulatory framework is fundamentally incomplete
and that the primary objective in the near term is legislative development, not construction.
All cost and timeline estimates are highly speculative pending nuclear law adoption.
